\section{Introduction}
Across Western Europe, Spain is a representative case of the demographic pressures currently facing developed economies. Population ageing, driven by increasing life expectancy and falling fertility rates, affects several economic and social outcomes. It places pressure on PAYG pension systems \parencite{diaz2009delaying,boeri2024pay}, increases public expenditure on health services \parencite{cristea2020impact,aiyar2016impact,bijak2007population}, and raises broader concerns about social security sustainability \parencite{failde2021perspective}. While social security and public services often make the headlines, there is little doubt that the labour market is the first point of impact from which many of these other effects cascade.

\vspace{0.7em}\par
Ageing changes the relative size of the age groups that contribute to aggregate labour supply \parencite{maestas2023effect}. This affects the number of potential workers, their participation rates, hours worked, and ultimately the contribution base that finances pensions, health care, and other public services. In this sense, the fiscal consequences of ageing are partly the downstream result of labour-market changes generated by demographic ageing. This article integrates the main demographic and behavioural components into a unified framework that isolates the contribution of each factor to changes in effective labour supply. The method measures aggregate labour supply as total potential annual hours worked, combining population size, age structure, labour-force participation, and hours worked by age group and birthplace status. Changes in labour supply are then decomposed into demographic, participation, and work-intensity components using a continuous-change decomposition model \parencite{horiuchiDecompositionMethodBased2008}.



\subsection{Ageing and Labour-Supply Pressure}

In Spain, this demographic pressure operates within a labour market already marked by structural unemployment, institutional segmentation, high sensitivity to economic cycles, the grey economy, and internal redistribution of human capital between natives and immigrants \parencite{bentolila1990mismatch,bentolila2010two,congregado2014unemployment,guell2021so,bentolila2020dual}. These features matter because they shape how demographic change is translated into labour supply. Ageing does not affect the labour market only by increasing the number of older individuals; it also changes the relative weight of age groups with different participation rates, employment prospects, and hours worked.

\vspace{0.7em}\par
Direct estimates for Spain confirm this composition effect. Banco de Espa{\~n}a estimates that population ageing reduced Spain's aggregate participation rate by about 3.4 percentage points in 2022. Without the ageing observed since 2012, the participation rate would have been 62.1\% rather than 58.7\%, equivalent to roughly 790,000 additional potential workers. The same study projects a further decline of about 2.8 percentage points by 2030 due to ageing, with a larger fall in the absence of net immigration \parencite{bde2023ageingParticipation}. CaixaBank Research reaches a similar conclusion: because participation follows an inverted-U pattern over the life cycle, the shift of the Spanish population toward older ages lowers the aggregate activity rate even when age-specific participation rates remain stable. It further projects that Spain's labour force could decline by around 1.7 million people between 2016 and 2029, with the activity rate falling to 53.8\%, although higher educational attainment among younger cohorts may partly offset the decline \parencite{caixabank2017labourForceAgeing}.

\vspace{0.7em}\par
More generally, international evidence shows that population ageing affects labour supply through both demographic composition and older-worker behaviour. Maestas, Mullen, and Powell show that ageing reduces labour-force growth and productivity growth, thereby lowering economic growth \parencite{maestas2023effect}. In the Spanish context, the OECD emphasizes that the effect of ageing depends not only on the number of older individuals but also on their ability and incentives to remain active in the labour market. Although participation among older workers has improved recently, Spain continues to face low employment rates among older individuals and institutional barriers to longer working lives \parencite{oecd2025olderWorkersMigrantsSpain}. Together, these studies suggest that the labour-market effect of ageing is twofold: it reduces the relative size of high-participation age groups while making aggregate labour supply increasingly dependent on the participation, employability, and working conditions of older workers.

\subsection{Labour force and inmigrant inflows}

In Western political and economic cultures, few questions are as contested, and yet as consequential, as immigration. For Spain, this debate is not abstract: immigration has become one of the forces through which the future of the labour market will be decided. After shifting from a country of emigration to a major destination in the early 2000s, Spain experienced large pre-crisis inflows that substantially rejuvenated its working-age population \parencite{amuedo2008complements,arango2013exceptional,gonzalez2011very}. In 2008 alone, Spain received 599,074 immigrants, with immigrants representing 13.9\% of the working-age population. These inflows matter because they expand the labour force at precisely the moment when population ageing is narrowing it. In this sense, immigration appears not simply as a demographic fact, but as one of the central margins through which Spain may sustain labour supply, competitiveness, and economic growth in an ageing society \parencite{stepanek2022sectoral,okamoto2021immigration,united2000replacement,casares2018labor}.

\vspace{0.7em}\par
However, the compensating role of immigration is not automatic, since immigrant labour supply is highly exposed to economic and political cycles. The 2008 financial crisis illustrates this vulnerability: foreign workers bore a disproportionate share of job losses, immigrant employment declined, and immigration inflows from abroad fell by more than 50\% between 2008 and 2013 \parencite{rodriguez2016labor}. By 2013, the immigrant share of the labour market had fallen to around 11\%, and Spain experienced net migration outflows. Although immigration recovered after 2014 and foreign arrivals continued to rise after the COVID-19 crisis, these fluctuations show that immigration cannot be treated as a stable or automatic offset to population ageing. In addition, by expanding the population, migration may increase demand for public services and affect welfare spending \parencite{fiorio2023migration,naumann2021population}. Its net effect therefore depends on migrants' age composition, labour-market attachment, employment stability, and use of public services \parencite{preston2014effect}.

\vspace{0.7em}\par
The key question is thus not simply whether immigration increases the size of the labour force, but whether it sufficiently increases labour supply through age composition, participation, and hours worked. On the one hand, immigration can help offset population ageing by enlarging the working-age population and increasing the number of potential contributors. On the other hand, its contribution depends on migrants' labour-market integration, exposure to economic cycles, age profile, and use of public services. The key question is thus not simply whether immigration increases population size, but whether it increases effective labour supply through age composition, participation, and hours worked.

\subsection{The missing link: an integrated framework}

Spain offers a timely and policy-relevant context for studying how demographic ageing, individual behavioural change, and immigration jointly shape aggregate labour supply. Existing studies establish that ageing and immigration are central to Spain's labour-market dynamics, but they often examine these forces separately. Research on ageing tends to emphasize pensions, health spending, social security, or the shrinking labour force \parencite{maestas2023effect,bde2023ageingParticipation,caixabank2017labourForceAgeing}, while research on immigration often focuses on migration flows, employment outcomes, integration, or fiscal effects \parencite{rodriguez2016labor,gonzalez2011very,preston2014effect}. Less attention has been given to how ageing, immigration, participation behaviour, and hours worked jointly shape aggregate labour supply.

\vspace{0.7em}\par
This gap matters because demographic change alone does not determine labour supply. Ageing may reduce labour supply by increasing the relative weight of older age groups, but immigration can change the size and age composition of the working-age population, while behavioural responses can alter participation and work intensity. These channels are especially relevant in Spain, where labour-market volatility has produced different responses across groups. During the Great Recession, for example, female participation increased through the added-worker effect \parencite{congregado2014unemployment,sanz2020labor}, while immigrant employment declined and reduced the foreign workforce \parencite{rodriguez2016labor}. Such patterns show that behavioural responses can either reinforce or offset demographic trends.

\vspace{0.7em}\par
This article addresses that problem through an integrated framework that links age structure, participation behaviour, hours worked, and migration-driven population change. It contributes by providing a more precise measure of aggregate labour supply, based on participation rates and hours worked by age group and residence status for the population aged 15 and over, and by decomposing changes in aggregate labour supply into demographic and behavioural components. This approach is important because it identifies whether observed labour-supply changes are driven mainly by structural demographic shifts, behavioural responses, or immigration inflows, thereby moving beyond broad claims about “ageing” or “migration” and providing evidence that is more useful for policy design.
